The single most common question first-time business buyers ask is: "How do I pay for this?" For most acquisitions of established small businesses, the answer is the SBA 7(a) loan.

## What Is an SBA 7(a) Loan?

The 7(a) loan is the Small Business Administration's flagship lending program. The SBA doesn't lend directly — it guarantees a portion of loans made by participating banks, which reduces the bank's risk and makes them willing to finance business acquisitions they otherwise wouldn't.

## How Much Can You Borrow?

SBA 7(a) loans go up to $5 million. For acquisitions, you can typically finance up to 90% of the purchase price, meaning a down payment as low as 10%.

## What Are the Requirements?

Lenders generally look for a buyer with relevant experience, a reasonable credit score, and enough cash for the down payment. Critically, the business being acquired must have strong enough cash flow to comfortably service the debt — which is exactly why established, profitable businesses from retiring owners are so attractive to lenders.

## How Seller Financing Helps

Many retiring owners will carry a seller note for a portion of the price. The SBA often allows this seller financing to count toward part of your equity injection, reducing the cash you personally need to bring to closing.

## What Sellers Need to Provide

To get a 7(a) loan approved, the lender will want three years of business tax returns and financial statements, a clear explanation of the business operations, and details on assets being transferred. This is another reason retiring owners with clean books make financing so much smoother.

## The Bottom Line

For most buyers, the SBA 7(a) loan turns "I can't afford to buy a business" into "I can buy a business that pays for itself." Combined with seller financing from a retiring owner, it's the most accessible path to ownership.
