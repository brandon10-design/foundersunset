If you're a retiring owner preparing to sell, the first question on your mind is probably "What's my business worth?" Understanding valuation before you go to market helps you price realistically and recognize a fair offer when it comes.

## The Most Common Method: SDE Multiples

For most small businesses, valuation starts with Seller's Discretionary Earnings (SDE) — essentially your net profit plus the owner's salary, benefits, and any one-time or personal expenses run through the business. Buyers then apply a multiple to this number.

Multiples typically range from 2x to 4x SDE for small businesses, depending on industry, growth, customer concentration, and how dependent the business is on you personally.

## Larger Businesses Use EBITDA

Once a business reaches a few million in revenue, buyers often shift to EBITDA (Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization) multiples instead of SDE.

## What Drives Your Multiple Up

- **Recurring revenue** and long-term contracts
- **Low customer concentration** — no single client dominating
- **Documented systems** so the business runs without you
- **Consistent or growing profits** over several years
- **A capable team** that will stay after the sale

## What Drives It Down

- Heavy dependence on the owner
- Declining revenue
- Customer concentration
- Messy or unverifiable financials

## Get a Professional Valuation

While these rules of thumb help you set expectations, a professional business valuation gives you a defensible number to take to market. It also reassures buyers and their lenders.

## The Bottom Line

Knowing your value before you list prevents both leaving money on the table and scaring off buyers with an unrealistic price. Clean financials and reduced owner-dependence are the two biggest levers you can pull before selling.
